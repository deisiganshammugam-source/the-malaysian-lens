Malaysia's Semiconductor Golden Goose Is Running Out of Time

A RM1 billion ARM Holdings deal. Institutional uncertainty. Two scenarios — both bad for Malaysia. Don't be tired. Be vigilant.

Semiconductors FDI DOSM By Deisigan  ·  March 2026

Malaysia's semiconductor industry is a golden goose.

But the goose is tired. And right now, the things that should be protecting it are doing the opposite.

Everyone knows Malaysia is a semiconductor powerhouse. It's our single biggest export category. Penang is known globally as the Silicon Valley of the East. We are proud of this. We should be.

But here's what the headline number hides.

We only test and package the chips. We don't design them. We don't manufacture them. The value-added work — the intellectual property, the engineering, the margins — happens somewhere else.

This is not a secret. Policymakers know it. MIDA knows it. The data flags it clearly. The question is: what are we doing about it?

The government had a plan to change this. RM1 billion. ARM Holdings — the British company whose chip designs power literally every smartphone on the planet — agreed to partner with Malaysia to train Malaysian engineers to design chips. ARM doesn't do deals like this with just anyone.

"ARM doesn't partner for prestige. They partner when they see a pipeline. That deal was Malaysia signalling it was serious about moving up the value chain."

Then came the uncertainty.

Bloomberg published an investigation raising questions about how certain institutions operate. Rafizi raised similar concerns publicly. Shortly after — a high-profile MACC case was filed against him. The case involves the ARM deal.

· · ·

Now think about what both scenarios mean for Malaysia.

If the allegations are true — we have a serious corruption problem at the heart of a RM1 billion strategic investment. That's devastating. Not just morally. Economically. FDI is built on trust in institutions.
If the allegations are NOT true — then we just spooked one of the world's most important tech companies for nothing. The chilling effect on ARM and every other potential partner is real, regardless of the outcome.

Both scenarios are bad for Malaysia.

Note the realisation rate. Approved FDI means a company said "yes, we intend to invest." Realised FDI means the money actually arrived. The gap between those two numbers is institutional confidence.

· · ·

I know what some of you are thinking.

"What's new. We've seen this before. 1MDB."

I get it. That exhaustion is real and valid.

But that exhaustion is exactly what allows these things to keep happening.

Don't be tired. Be vigilant. Ask questions. Demand answers.
Malaysia has come too far to lose it to institutional uncertainty — real or perceived.
