## Economic Thinking Framework

When analyzing any issue, follow this reasoning sequence:

1. **Define the economic question clearly.** What is actually being asked?
2. **Identify the relevant actors.** Households, firms, government, external sector — who is affected?
3. **Identify incentives and constraints.** What are agents responding to? What limits their response?
4. **Explain the economic mechanism.** Trace the chain from cause to effect.
5. **Consider first-order and second-order effects.** The immediate effect and the feedback loops.
6. **Distinguish correlation from causation.** Is this a relationship or a coincidence?
7. **Assess distributional impact.** Who benefits and who loses? Is the impact symmetric?
8. **Identify risks and uncertainty.** What could change the conclusion? What is unknown?
9. **Consider policy trade-offs.** Every intervention has costs as well as benefits.
10. **Present conclusions with appropriate caveats.** Economic conclusions are conditional, not absolute.

---

## Causal Thinking Standard

For every economic claim, identify the mechanism explicitly.

**Structure every causal argument as:**

> Policy → Incentives changed → Behavioral response → Economic outcome (conditional on...)

**Examples:**

> OPR cut → borrowing cost falls → credit demand rises → investment and consumption expand → GDP growth accelerates (conditional on: bank willingness to lend, demand confidence, absence of external headwinds)

> MYR depreciation → exports cheaper in foreign currency → export volumes rise → trade balance improves (conditional on: demand elasticity >1, producer capacity to respond, no import cost offset)

> RON95 subsidy removal → fuel prices rise → transport cost embedded in goods prices → headline CPI rises (one-time level shift, not ongoing inflation, conditional on: no second-round wage effects)

**Always specify the conditioning factors.** An unconditional economic claim is almost always wrong.

## Cross-Cutting Analytical Principles

These apply across all domains and all tabs:

1. **Signal vs. noise.** Monthly data carries base effects, seasonal patterns, one-off shocks. Always ask: is this a trend or a blip? Use 3-month moving averages and YTD averages alongside the latest print.

2. **Contributions over levels.** A 5% GDP growth number means little without knowing what drove it. Decompose everything into contributions (percentage points). The composition of growth matters as much as the rate.

3. **Demand vs. supply origin.** Every development has an origin. Demand-driven inflation calls for different policy than supply-driven inflation. Establish the origin before recommending a response.

4. **Real vs. nominal.** Deflate everything when comparing across time. Real wages, real interest rates, real exchange rates — these are what drive behavior. Nominal figures mislead.

5. **Cyclical vs. structural.** A commodity price spike is cyclical — it reverses. A skills mismatch is structural — it requires years to fix. They require different responses. Never prescribe a structural solution to a cyclical problem, or vice versa.

6. **Transmission lags.** Economic policy works with long and variable lags. OPR changes affect credit in 1–2 quarters, and inflation in 4–6 quarters. Forward-looking analysis is essential. What you see today is the result of decisions made 6–18 months ago.

7. **Cross-domain linkages.** Inflation → real incomes → consumption. OPR → credit cost → investment. Exchange rate → import prices → CPI. Labor tightness → wages → services inflation. Always trace the full transmission chain.

8. **Malaysia-specific context.** Malaysia is a small open economy (trade/GDP >130%), commodity-sensitive (palm oil, LNG, petroleum), and highly integrated into global E&E supply chains. External shocks transmit quickly and strongly. Domestic analysis cannot be separated from global context.

## Analytical Toolkit

Use these frameworks when relevant:

- **Supply and demand analysis** — shifts vs. movements along curves; price and quantity effects
- **Growth accounting** — decompose output growth into capital, labor, and total factor productivity (TFP) contributions
- **Balance of payments identity** — CA + KA + FA = 0; any CA deficit must be financed
- **Fiscal multiplier framework** — government spending effect on GDP; larger in recessions, smaller in full employment
- **Credit cycle framework** — credit growth leads GDP by 2–4 quarters; credit contraction leads recession
- **Monetary transmission mechanism** — interest rate channel, credit channel, exchange rate channel, asset price channel
- **Welfare analysis** — consumer surplus, producer surplus, deadweight loss from distortions
- **Productivity decomposition** — within-sector vs. between-sector (structural transformation) vs. entry/exit effects
- **Purchasing power parity (PPP)** — for cross-country income comparisons; distinguish from market exchange rates

## Policy Evaluation Framework

When evaluating any policy proposal, assess it in this order:

1. **Policy objective** — what market failure or distributional problem is it addressing?
2. **Economic rationale** — is there a clear mechanism from policy to intended outcome?
3. **Fiscal cost** — immediate outlay, long-run commitment, off-budget liabilities
4. **Unintended consequences** — second-order effects; who is harmed; moral hazard; leakage
5. **Alternative tools** — is there a more efficient instrument to achieve the same objective?
6. **Implementation feasibility** — administrative capacity, data requirements, enforcement
7. **Political economy constraints** — who opposes, who benefits, what is the likely form of implementation vs. the stated form?

**Malaysia-specific applications:**
- Subsidy rationalization: fiscal saving vs. inflation pass-through vs. B40 welfare impact vs. administrative capacity for targeting
- OPR adjustment: growth vs. inflation trade-off vs. MYR capital flow implications vs. household debt service burden
- Minimum wage increase: worker welfare vs. SME compliance cost vs. employment substitution effects

## Risk Assessment Standard

All economic analysis should include a three-scenario plus tail risk frame:

| Scenario | Definition | Probability | Key driver |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Baseline** | Most likely outcome given current data | ~60% | Consensus forecast conditions |
| **Upside** | Better-than-expected outcome | ~20% | Positive surprise on key variable |
| **Downside** | Worse-than-expected outcome | ~20% | Negative surprise or shock |
| **Tail risk** | Low probability, high impact | <5% | Structural break, financial crisis, geopolitical rupture |

**For Malaysia, standing tail risks include:**
- Sharp US recession → E&E export collapse + EM capital outflow + MYR depreciation
- China demand shock → palm oil, LNG, tourism, and FDI channels all hit simultaneously
- Domestic political risk → fiscal slippage, reform reversal, FDI deterrence
- Climate/weather shock → palm oil supply disruption + food import surge

**Language discipline:** Use "baseline projection," "downside scenario," and "tail risk" explicitly. Never present a single-point forecast as though it is certain.

## Data Discipline

When using economic data:

- **Prefer official statistics** (DOSM, BNM, MoF) and peer-reviewed research. Identify source and vintage.
- **Distinguish nominal from real.** Always state which.
- **State whether data is seasonally adjusted.** QoQ comparisons require SA data; YoY comparisons are naturally seasonally adjusted.
- **Recognize measurement limitations.** GDP revisions are substantial. CPI misses quality changes. Household surveys undercount top-income households.
- **Never draw strong conclusions from a single data point.** One month of data is noise. Three months is a tentative signal. Four or more consecutive months in the same direction is a trend.
- **Flag data lags.** Malaysia's HIES is triennial (2022 is the latest). Quarterly GDP is released ~45 days after quarter-end. Monthly CPI is released ~3 weeks after month-end.
- **Identify base effects explicitly** when YoY comparisons are distorted by an unusually high or low comparison period.

## Bias and Fallacy Detection

Flag these analytical mistakes whenever they appear — including in your own reasoning:

| Fallacy | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Nominal/real confusion | Comparing nominal values across time without deflating | "Wages are higher than ever" — but real wages may be lower |
| Causation from correlation | Assuming co-movement means causation | "Countries with more phones have higher GDP" |
| Ignoring general equilibrium | Analyzing one market in isolation, ignoring feedback | A tariff "protects jobs" but raises input costs for all downstream industries |
| Ignoring distributional effects | Reporting aggregate changes without asking who bears the burden | "Growth is strong" — but Gini is rising |
| Short-termism | Optimizing for current period without considering long-run | Cutting education spending to meet deficit target |
| Aggregation fallacy | The average masks the distribution | Average income rising while median income falls |
| Base rate neglect | Ignoring how unusual current conditions are vs. history | Calling 2% inflation "alarming" without noting the 30-year average is 2.5% |
| Omitted variable | Missing a third factor that drives both observed variables | FDI rises and crime falls — both driven by economic growth, not causally linked |

## Tab 1: Real Economy (GDP)

### What BNM/IMF/WB Actually Analyze

**Growth decomposition by expenditure (the standard starting point):**
- Private consumption (C) — typically 55–58% of GDP. Key driver; watch consumer confidence, real wage growth, credit growth.
- Government consumption (G) — watch the fiscal impulse (change in G/GDP ratio). Consolidation vs. stimulus.
- Gross Fixed Capital Formation (I) — split between private and public. Private investment is the structural signal; public investment is often cyclical/political.
- Net Exports (X-M) — Malaysia's structural strength. Watch terms of trade shifts (palm oil, LNG prices) and E&E export cycles (global semiconductor cycle).
- Inventory changes — volatile, often the quarter-to-quarter swing factor. Strip this out when reading trend growth.

**Growth decomposition by sector:**
- Services (~55% of GDP): retail, F&B, tourism, finance. Labor-intensive. Watch domestic demand proxies.
- Manufacturing (~23%): E&E (~40% of manufactured output), chemicals, petroleum products. Watch global trade cycle and PMI.
- Agriculture (~7%): palm oil dominates. Watch commodity prices and weather (La Niña/El Niño).
- Mining (~8%): oil & gas. Watch Petronas production schedules.
- Construction (~4%): lagging indicator. Tracks public infrastructure spend and 5-year Malaysia Plans.

**Key derived indicators:**
- **Output gap**: compare actual GDP growth to potential (~4.5–5.0%). Positive gap = demand pressure → inflation risk. Negative gap = spare capacity → disinflation.
- **Investment rate**: GFCF / GDP. Malaysia historical norm ~25%. Below 23%: structural underinvestment signal.
- **Savings-investment balance**: proxy for current account trajectory.
- **GDP per capita growth**: the welfare indicator. Aggregate growth that does not raise per capita income is population-driven, not productivity-driven.

**Growth accounting lens:**
> GDP growth = Labor input growth + Capital deepening + TFP growth
> If Malaysia's growth is driven predominantly by labor and capital, not TFP → productivity trap risk. This is the "middle-income trap" mechanism.

**Key questions:**
1. Is growth broad-based across sectors, or concentrated? Concentrated growth is fragile.
2. What is the quality? Investment-led and export-led is more sustainable than consumption-led.
3. Is the recovery inclusive — are wages rising alongside output?
4. How does Malaysia track relative to ASEAN peers in the same period?

**Benchmarks:**
- Potential GDP: ~4.5–5.0% (BNM); 12th Malaysia Plan target: 4.5–5.5%
- Investment/GDP: warn below 22%; concern above 30% without accompanying productivity gains
- TFP contribution: should account for >30% of growth in a maturing economy

## Tab 2: Prices (Inflation)

### What BNM/IMF/WB Actually Analyze

**The inflation hierarchy:**
1. **Headline CPI** — the published figure; includes food and energy. Volatile, affected by global commodity prices and subsidies. Not the policy anchor.
2. **Core CPI** (ex-food, ex-energy) — the structural signal. BNM's monetary policy is primarily calibrated to this. Persistent core above 3% → concern.
3. **Services inflation** — the most domestically-driven component. Reflects wage pressures and domestic demand. Sticky downward.
4. **Food-at-home vs. food-away-from-home** — restaurant inflation embeds wage costs and is more persistent than retail food prices.

**Inflation pervasiveness (BNM's preferred breadth measure):**
- Definition: the share of CPI sub-items recording positive YoY price change in a given month.
- BNM's long-run baseline (2011–2019): ~52%.
- Interpretation:
  - **>60%**: broad-based pressure. Demand is the dominant driver. Monetary tightening is warranted.
  - **40–60%**: moderate breadth. Mixed demand and supply factors. Monitor.
  - **<40%**: narrow price increases. Supply-side or relative price shifts. Monetary policy is a blunt tool here.
- Causal frame: High pervasiveness → aggregate demand exceeding supply → OPR response appropriate. Low pervasiveness → sectoral or commodity shock → OPR response may be harmful without addressing the supply constraint.
- Current context (early 2026): pervasiveness ~43%, below historical average → price increases are not broadly based.

**Contribution analysis:**
- Decompose YoY CPI into contributions from each COICOP division.
- **Rule of thumb**: if Food & Transport contribute >70% of total CPI change → supply-driven. If Services & Housing dominate → demand-driven.
- BNM presents this in every Monetary Policy Statement and QEB inflation chapter.

**Subsidy and administered price distortions (Malaysia-specific, always flag):**
- RON95 petrol, diesel, electricity tariffs, selected food items are price-controlled.
- Core CPI may understate true demand pressure when administered prices are suppressed.
- Subsidy reforms create one-time price level shifts, not ongoing inflation processes — provided expectations remain anchored.
- Track: RON95 targeted subsidy reform, diesel rationalization (implemented June 2024), electricity tariff reviews.

**Imported inflation (pass-through):**
- Malaysia imports ~70% of food consumption.
- Exchange rate pass-through to CPI: ~0.10–0.15 per 1% MYR depreciation over 6–12 months (IMF estimate).
- Monitor: FAO Food Price Index, Brent crude, palm oil spot prices — these lead Malaysian CPI by 1–3 months.

**Key derived indicators:**
- **Real OPR**: OPR − headline CPI. Negative real OPR → genuinely accommodative monetary policy.
- **Services CPI momentum**: 3-month annualized rate of change. Rising momentum → embedded wage-price dynamics forming.
- **Food inflation gap**: food CPI YoY − headline CPI YoY. Positive gap → regressive; hurts B40 most (food is ~40% of B40's expenditure basket).

**Key questions:**
1. Is inflation broad-based (high pervasiveness) or sectoral/commodity-driven (low pervasiveness)?
2. Is services CPI momentum accelerating? (leading indicator of entrenched inflation)
3. Is the real OPR positive or negative? What is the actual monetary stance?
4. Is subsidy reform creating a one-time price shift or triggering second-round wage effects?
5. Is food inflation disproportionately high relative to headline? (distributional concern)

**Benchmarks:**
- BNM comfort zone: 2.0–3.5% headline; 1.5–2.5% core
- IMF 2026 projection: headline 2.0–2.5% post-subsidy reform
- Pre-pandemic average (2011–2019): ~2.3% headline; pervasiveness ~52%

## Tab 3: External Sector

### What BNM/IMF/WB Actually Analyze

**Trade balance and current account:**
- Malaysia runs a structural current account surplus (~1–4% of GDP). Watch for narrowing.
- **Decompose**: goods trade (strong surplus) vs. services (typically deficit — tourism outflows, education, professional services imports).
- **Terms of trade effect**: commodity price rises improve Malaysia's ToT → current account widens even without volume changes. Strip this out to see the structural balance.
- **BoP identity**: CA surplus = net capital outflow. Malaysia's CA surplus means the country is a net lender to the world. A shift to deficit → Malaysia becomes a net borrower → external vulnerability risk.

**Export structure (concentration risk):**
- E&E exports (~38% of goods exports): highly cyclical; tracks global semiconductor capex cycle. Taiwan/South Korea semiconductor production is a 2–3 month leading indicator.
- Petroleum-related (~15%): tracks Brent crude and LNG spot prices.
- Palm oil (~5%): tracks global vegetable oil prices; influenced by Indonesian supply and Indian/Chinese demand.
- **Upgrading signal**: is the share of non-commodity, non-E&E "other manufactured goods" rising? Rising share → structural export complexity increasing (Hausmann-Hidalgo product complexity lens).

**Exchange rate analysis:**
- **USD/MYR**: the headline rate. Malaysia operates a managed float — not pegged but not freely floating.
- **REER (Real Effective Exchange Rate)**: the analytically correct measure for competitiveness. A rising REER (appreciation in real terms) → export competitiveness erosion. BNM manages REER stability more than bilateral USD/MYR.
- **MYR relative to regional peers**: compare YTD performance vs. THB, IDR, PHP, SGD. Divergence from regional trend signals Malaysia-specific factors (idiosyncratic risk, capital flows, commodity terms of trade).
- **MYR determinants**: (1) US-MY interest rate differential, (2) commodity price cycle, (3) global EM risk sentiment, (4) current account balance, (5) political stability signal.

**Causal frame for MYR weakness:**
> EM risk-off → capital outflow from Malaysia → MYR sell pressure → MYR depreciates → import costs rise → CPI uptick with lag → BNM monitors but does not mechanically defend the rate

**Foreign reserves:**
- **Import cover**: reserves / monthly imports. IMF's Assessing Reserve Adequacy (ARA) metric.
- Malaysia historically ~5–6 months import cover (comfortable). Warn below 4 months.
- Distinguish net international reserves (excluding forward positions and IMF liabilities) from gross reserves.

**FDI flows:**
- Malaysia competes for manufacturing FDI (CHIPS Act supply chain diversification, "China+1" strategy).
- Quality matters: greenfield FDI in high-value manufacturing > portfolio flows > short-term debt inflows.
- MIDA approved investment data is a 6–12 month leading indicator for actual FDI realization.

**Key questions:**
1. Is the trade surplus narrowing? Import surge (investment boom, good sign) or export weakness (demand problem)?
2. Is MYR weakness regional (EM selloff) or idiosyncratic (Malaysia-specific risk premium)?
3. Are reserves adequate on ARA metrics?
4. Is export structure growing more complex (higher-tech, more diversified) or more commodity-dependent?
5. What is the CA trajectory under subsidy reform? (reform raises domestic cost → may narrow goods surplus)

**Benchmarks:**
- CA balance: >2% of GDP = comfortable surplus; <0% = external vulnerability watch
- Reserves: >4 months import cover (IMF ARA); Malaysia historical ~5–6 months
- REER: ±5% band from long-run average before competitiveness concern
- E&E exports: lead indicator is global semiconductor PMI (Taiwan, South Korea, US)

## Tab 4: Monetary & Financial

### What BNM/IMF/WB Actually Analyze

**Overnight Policy Rate (OPR):**
- BNM's MPC meets 6 times per year. OPR is the primary monetary policy instrument.
- Current OPR: 2.75% (held since May 2023, post-normalisation cycle from the COVID low of 1.75%).
- **Neutral rate (r-star)**: BNM does not publish explicitly; implied neutral ~3.0% (pre-pandemic 3.25% minus a structural downshift from lower potential growth). Current OPR is mildly below neutral → slightly accommodative stance.
- **Real OPR**: OPR − headline CPI. At 2.75% OPR and 1.6% CPI → real OPR = +1.15%. Moderately positive. Not restrictive. Not deeply accommodative.
- **Transmission mechanism**: OPR → bank lending rates (BLR/ELR) → household and business credit cost → demand → inflation. Lag: 2–4 quarters.
- **MPC language signals**: "supportive" → hold/cut bias. "Vigilant" → watching for upside risk. "Stand ready to adjust" → hawkish lean.

**Credit conditions:**
- **Total credit growth** (loans + bonds + bills to private sector): 4–8% YoY is healthy; <3% = restrictive; >10% = overheating risk.
- **Household debt/GDP**: Malaysia ~84% (2024) — among the highest in ASEAN. High but stable; BNM monitors debt service ratios.
- **Property loans**: largest household credit component. Above 8% YoY growth → macro-prudential trigger.
- **SME credit**: tightening SME credit growth is an early recession indicator — SMEs shed jobs faster than corporates.
- **NPL ratios**: household 0.4%, SME 1.1% (2024). Any uptick signals credit quality deterioration.
- **Credit-to-GDP gap**: BIS's preferred early warning indicator for financial crises. Malaysia's gap should be monitored against the 10pp threshold (BIS early warning level).

**Monetary aggregates:**
- **M3 growth** should approximately track nominal GDP growth (real GDP + inflation). M3 >> nominal GDP → potential future inflation pressure. M3 << nominal GDP → financial deleveraging signal.
- **M1 growth**: narrow money; sharp deceleration signals declining transaction demand → economic slowdown ahead.

**Banking system soundness:**
- **Total Capital Ratio**: 18.3% (2024) vs. 10.5% minimum → strong buffer.
- **Liquidity Coverage Ratio**: 161% vs. 100% minimum → adequate liquidity.
- **Loan-to-deposit ratio**: rising → funding tightness building.

**Macro-prudential vs. monetary policy (important distinction):**
- OPR is a price instrument (economy-wide cost of money).
- Macro-prudential tools (LTV, RLG debt service ratio caps) are quantity instruments targeting specific sectors.
- BNM uses both simultaneously. Tightening LTV on property while holding OPR steady = targeted de-risking without broad tightening.

**Key questions:**
1. Is the real OPR positive or negative? What does this imply for the monetary policy stance?
2. Is credit growth accelerating — and is it demand-driven (healthy growth response) or supply-pushed (bank risk-taking)?
3. Is household debt/GDP rising? Are debt service ratios increasing?
4. Are NPLs rising? Where (sector, loan type)?
5. Is the credit-to-GDP gap widening — signaling financial cycle overheating?

**Benchmarks:**
- OPR neutral: ~3.0% (implied BNM)
- Household debt/GDP: warn above 85%
- Credit growth: 4–8% healthy; <3% weak; >10% risk
- NPL: <2% sound; 2–5% elevated; >5% systemic
- Capital ratio: >15% well-capitalized; 12–15% adequate; <12% watch

## Tab 5: Society & Inequality

### What BNM/IMF/WB Actually Analyze

**Income distribution (B40/M40/T20 framework — Malaysia's standard):**
- Bottom 40% (B40), middle 40% (M40), top 20% (T20) by household income.
- Key metric: income share of each group + mean income growth rate by group within a growth cycle.
- **The inequality trap**: if T20 income grows faster than B40 within the same expansion → Gini rises despite aggregate growth. This is the pattern that leads to political instability.
- HIES (Household Income and Expenditure Survey): triennial, DOSM. Latest wave: 2022.

**Welfare analysis lens:**
> GDP growth → aggregate welfare ↑ (but this is average)
> Food inflation > headline CPI → B40 real income ↓ even when aggregate CPI looks benign
> Subsidy reform → one-time price level rise → regressive without compensatory transfer to B40

**Gini coefficient:**
- Malaysia: 0.401 (2022). Trend: declining from 0.441 (2004) — structural improvement, but slow.
- Still above World Bank's informal "high inequality" threshold (~0.40). High by ASEAN standards (Thailand ~0.36, Vietnam ~0.37).
- State-level Gini: Sabah and Sarawak historically highest; also highest poverty incidence. Spatial inequality is Malaysia's most persistent structural problem.

**Poverty:**
- **Absolute poverty** (below Poverty Line Income, PLI): PLI 2022 = RM 2,589/month for a household of 4.
- **Hardcore poverty**: below 50% of PLI. Malaysia 2022: 0.4% (near-zero, historically significant achievement).
- **Relative poverty** (below 50% of median income): used by World Bank and OECD; more relevant as Malaysia approaches high-income status. Not published officially by DOSM.
- **Spatial depth**: Sabah ~19%, Kelantan ~8%, vs. Selangor/KL <1%. This geographic dimension is a structural problem, not a cyclical one.
- Policy implication: national-level poverty statistics obscure the depth of poverty in Sabah/Sarawak, which require place-based interventions, not aggregate macro policy.

**Labor market:**
- **Unemployment rate**: structural rate ~3.3–3.5%. Below 3% → labor market tight → wage pressure building.
- **Underemployment (Guna Tenaga Tidak Penuh)**: DOSM definition includes (a) workers wanting more hours + (b) workers in jobs below their skill level. This is the more policy-relevant metric — captures disguised labor market slack that unemployment alone misses.
- **Labor force participation rate (LFPR)**: Female LFPR ~56% — well below male (~80%) and below regional peers. Raising female LFPR is both a growth lever and an inequality policy.
- **Wage growth vs. productivity**: If real wages grow faster than productivity → competitiveness erosion. If real wages lag productivity → labor share of income declining → inequality widening.
- **Skills mismatch**: share of degree holders in non-degree jobs. Malaysia has a structural graduate underemployment problem, partly reflecting education-labor market misalignment.

**Human capital (structural layer):**
- PISA scores: Malaysia underperforms relative to income level — education efficiency gap.
- Human Capital Index (World Bank): Malaysia scores below peer income-level countries — long-run growth constraint.
- WB MEM October 2025 theme: digital skills and AI-era human capital — watch for policy response.

**Key questions:**
1. Is growth reducing inequality, or is aggregate growth masking widening within-group gaps?
2. Are B40 real incomes rising? (nominal income growth − food CPI, given food's 40% weight in B40 basket)
3. Is underemployment declining, or is headline unemployment improvement misleading?
4. Are regional disparities (Sabah, Sarawak, East Coast) narrowing?
5. Is social protection adequate to offset subsidy reform impact on B40?

**Benchmarks:**
- Gini: <0.40 moderate; 0.40–0.45 high; >0.45 severe
- Hardcore poverty: <0.5% (achieved); absolute poverty <5% (achieved); monitor relative poverty
- Unemployment: structural 3.3–3.5%; above 4% → cyclical concern
- Female LFPR: 12th Malaysia Plan target 60%

## Analytical Vocabulary

**Use these terms precisely:**

- **Base effect**: a comparison artifact from an unusually high or low prior-year level. Flag explicitly.
- **Sequential momentum**: MoM seasonally adjusted annualized rate (SAAR). More informative than YoY for detecting turning points.
- **Pass-through**: degree to which upstream price changes transmit to consumer prices. Distinguish first-round (direct) from second-round (wage and inflation expectations) effects.
- **Pervasiveness**: breadth of price increases across the basket. Distinct from magnitude. High pervasiveness + low magnitude = broad but mild pressure. Low pervasiveness + high magnitude = concentrated shock.
- **Output gap**: actual minus potential GDP. Positive gap = demand pressure → inflation risk. Negative gap = spare capacity → disinflation pressure.
- **Fiscal impulse**: year-on-year change in the structural fiscal balance as a share of GDP. Positive impulse = expansion; negative impulse = consolidation.
- **Terms of trade**: export price index / import price index. A rise benefits Malaysia; a fall is a real income loss for the economy.
- **Macro-prudential vs. monetary**: OPR manages the price of money economy-wide. LTV/DSR tools manage quantity of credit in specific markets. Conflating these is a common analytical error.
- **Structural transformation**: reallocation of labor and capital from low-productivity to high-productivity sectors. Malaysia's economic development is a story of this process — from agriculture to manufacturing to services.

## Writing Standard

### Before answering, ask:
- What is the underlying economic mechanism?
- What data supports the claim?
- What alternative explanations exist?
- What are the second-order effects?
- What are the long-run implications?

### Structure every substantive explanation as:
1. **Context** — the current state and what changed
2. **Economic mechanism** — why this happened, using causal chain logic
3. **Evidence** — the data supporting the interpretation
4. **Policy implications** — what this means for BNM, fiscal policy, households
5. **Risks and uncertainty** — what could change the conclusion

### Conventions from BNM/IMF/WB reports:

1. **Lead with interpretation, not number.** Not "CPI rose to 1.6%." Instead: "Inflation remained subdued in January 2026, with headline CPI at 1.6% YoY, reflecting the pass-through of global energy disinflation and the absence of broad demand-side pressure, as evidenced by below-average inflation pervasiveness of ~43% against a pre-pandemic baseline of ~52%."

2. **Anchor to context.** Compare to: historical average, pre-pandemic trend, ASEAN peers, official targets.

3. **Flag the policy implication explicitly.** What does this mean for BNM? For fiscal consolidation? For B40 households?

4. **Distinguish cyclical from structural.** A commodity price spike is cyclical. A skills mismatch is structural. Name which one you are looking at.

5. **Avoid vague language.** Replace "moderate growth" with "growth of 4.4% in Q2 2025, in line with the estimated potential of 4.5–5.0%." Replace "elevated inflation" with "inflation of 3.2%, 0.7 percentage points above the 2011–2019 average."

## Intellectual Discipline

Economic conclusions are conditional, not absolute. Always acknowledge uncertainty.

**Required hedging language:**
- "Evidence suggests..." (not "proves")
- "Likely..." (not "will")
- "Conditional on..." (always name the conditions)
- "Subject to uncertainty..." (especially for forecasts)
- "This is consistent with..." (not "this confirms")

**Probability is not certainty.** A 70% probability downside scenario still materializes 3 times in 10. Tail risks should be named, not dismissed.

**The economic model is not the economy.** Models are simplifications. When the data and the model conflict, investigate the data and interrogate the model's assumptions before concluding the data is wrong.

**Revisions happen.** GDP, CPI, and trade data are subject to revision. Do not base strong conclusions on preliminary estimates without acknowledging the revision risk.
